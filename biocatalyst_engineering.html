Biocatalyst Engineering

A longstanding area of research in the Clark group has been the development of biocatalysts for use in non-traditional media. Transferring or improving an enzyme’s evolved catalytic properties for new media is an engineering challenge which can require enzyme surface modification, solvent tailoring, or genetic manipulations.

Many chemical transformations that are difficult or inefficient with traditional catalysts are easily performed by enzymes. However, the utility of enzymes as alternative catalysts is limited by their generally poor performance in conditions other than aqueous buffer. In particular, conditions which are favorable for chemical synthesis, such as organic solvents, high temperatures or pressures, are wholly unsuitable for enzymes, and here they perform orders of magnitude below their optima.

The reduced catalytic activity in media such as organic solvents is due to changes in enzyme properties that are critical to catalysis, including altered active site electronics, the slowing of protein motions that are relevant to catalysis, or structural loss. The Clark group has been successful in identifying these problems and devising ways to mitigate them. Primary tools for protein structural and dynamic characterization have been NMR, ESR, and fluorescence spectroscopy, and we have benefited greatly in this work from collaborations with Professors Jeff Reimer at UC Berkeley and Jonathan Dordick at Rensselaer Polytechnic.

Techniques for enhancing enzyme activity in organic solvents have been both simple and complex. For example, lyophilization of enzyme powders with non-buffer salts has been shown to increase enzyme active site polarity and protein flexibility. Properties of the additive salt, such as its ability to order surrounding water, can dramatically influence enzyme performance, increasing catalytic efficiencies up to 30,000-fold. Solubilization of enzymes into organic solvents using surfactant pairing presents a solution-phase version of a very active biocatalyst. Genetic engineering is another avenue for increasing enzyme activity. Single point mutations have been shown to alter an enzyme’s ability to accept a reaction cofactor. All of these development are steps forward in the eventual implementation of enzymes in the pharmaceutical, chemical, and agrochemical industries.
