Research Interests
Activation mechanisms for surfactant- solubilized enzymes in non-aqueous media

The removal of enzymes from their natural (aqueous) environs enables the extension and tailoring of their unrivaled specificity for synthetic chemistries of new substrates and reaction systems. While the promise of fresh and exciting uses for enzymes is alluring, reduced reaction rates and long term stability issues loom as barriers to the widespread implementation of non-aqueous biocatalysis on the industrial scale. Traditional explanations of the deleterious effects of organic solvents include the “tightening” of enzyme conformations in low dielectric media, the partial loss of tertiary and secondary structure, and solvent penetration into the active site (for a recent review, see [1].)

The methods used to increase enzyme activity, stability, and specificity in organic solvents include chemical modification of amino acid residues, co-lyophilization with inert salts, and protein engineering techniques such as directed evolution and rational mutagenisis. Despite some outstanding results, the mechanisms for activation are difficult to discriminate, and phenomenological explanations apply to many techniques.

My research in the Clark and Reimer labs is currently focused a particular activation method and protein, the ion-pairing of the serine protease Subtilisin Carlsberg with the amphiphilic surfactant Aerosol OT. The anionic head group of the surfactant pairs with charged residues on the enzyme surface, resulting in an organic soluble enzyme. Preparation of the ion-paired enzyme has recently been shown to give unexpected changes in substrate specificity when compared to the native suspension [2,3], a phenomenon which can be exploited in screening for novel drug derivatives. It has also been found in this lab that solubilization using two distinct methods gives drastically different transesterification rates in solvents of various polarity.

Nuclear Magnetic Resonance (NMR) spectroscopy of protein atoms allows for the investigation of their electronic environment as well as mobility. NMR can be directed toward motions of protein backbone atoms, surfactant side chains, and the extent of solvent intrusion into the catalytic triad active site.

By complimenting kinetic studies (kcat, Km parameters) with NMR, I hope to elucidate the molecular-level events that lead to enhanced (or reduced) ion-paired enzyme activity due to preparation technique and solvent properties.
