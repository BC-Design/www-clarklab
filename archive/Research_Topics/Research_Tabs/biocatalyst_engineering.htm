Biocatalyst Engineering:

Much of the Clark Group’s research has focused on the development of new enzyme systems with improved properties for practical applications, ranging from drug discovery to large-scale bioprocesses. Our group has devised many methods for greatly enhancing the activity and stability of enzymes under conditions suitable for the synthesis of chemicals and pharmaceuticals, e.g., at high temperatures and pressures, and/or in organic solvents. These developments are particularly important for the large-scale application of enzymes in the pharmaceutical, chemical, and agrochemical industries.

One technology of particular interest in nonaqueous biocatalysis, i.e., applications of enzymes in organic media or nearly anhydrous environments. Nonaqueous biocatalysis has the potential to greatly extend conventional aqueous-based biocatalysis; however, enzymes typically exhibit very low reaction rates in organic media, thus limiting their practical utility. In research aimed at improving enzyme function in low-water environments, we have shown that enzyme activity in organic solvents can be improved nearly 30,000-fold by including simple nonbuffer salts in the lyophilized enzyme. The groundwork for this discovery was laid by electron paramagnetic resonance (EPR) spectroscopy studies of enzyme structure-function relationships in organic media. We are now in the process of investigating the mechanism of this activation in greater detail, and expanding the approach to a wide range of synthetically useful enzymes.
