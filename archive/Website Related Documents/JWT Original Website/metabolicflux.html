Another focus of Professor Clarkâ€™s group is the development of novel approaches for discovering small molecule therapeutics against diseases such as cancer. His group currently uses computer modeling and experimental monitoring of metabolic reaction networks in normal and cancerous breast tissue, in order to identify potential metabolic drug targets. By feeding cells various 13C labeled nutrients and measuring where this isotope ends up within cellular biomass and waste products, it is possible to estimate the magnitude of hundreds of cellular reaction rates simultaneously. This information is used to better understand the metabolic alterations that occur within normal tissue on their progression to an aggressive cancer. It is our hope that the metabolic abnormalities identified by this method will suggest new ways in which cancer growth may be curtailed.

In addition, Professor Clark, in collaboration with Professor John Dordick at RPI, has developed a new technique for screening thousands of bio-catalytically generated drugs simultaneously on a small chip. This technology, termed the MetaChip, will be useful in mimicking the human liver (the organ primarily responsible for chemically modifying ingested pharmaceuticals) so that the toxicity of likely chemical derivatives may be assessed in vitro (see Figure left). In addition, the MetaChip may be used to simultaneously generate new drugs in a combinatorial fashion and rapidly assay their affect on a variety of tissues, thereby potentially increasing the speed at which new drugs can be developed.
