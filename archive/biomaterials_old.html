Research Interests Biomaterials and Biocatalytic Microsystems

Drug discovery is an arduous process taking nearly 13 years of research and nearly $1b for every successful drug that reaches the market. A major reason for this low success rate and high cost is because of the factors that are not typically considered in the early stages of drug discovery, including poor ADME/tox (absorption, distribution, metabolism, excretion, and toxicology) profiles. Thus, pharmaceutical companies are beginning to evaluate toxicity of drug candidates early in the discovery process to reduce the chances of late-stage failure.

We have addressed this issue with the development of a high-throughput miniaturized 3D cell-culture array (the Data Analysis Toxicology Assay Chip or DataChip) and a P450 enzyme array (the Metabolizing Enzyme Toxicology Assay Chip or MetaChip) for in vitro high-throughput toxicity screening of drug candidates and their cytochrome P450- generated metabolites. These chips are capable of working with volumes as low as 20 nL, which is more than 2000-fold reduction than what is currently used in the pharmaceutical industries.

The MetaChip consists of P450 enzymes encapsulated in a polymeric sol-gel matrix, spotted in an array format (PNAS, 2005, 102:983-987), and the DataChip consists of human cells encapsulated in a collagen or an alginate matrix, spotted in an array format complementary to the MetaChip. Both MetaChip and DataChip use glass slides treated specially for the attachment of the sol-gel or collagen/alginate matrices. We recently demonstrated (PNAS, 2008, 105:59-63) that the DataChip containing 1,080 individual cell cultures, used in conjunction with the complementary MetaChip, simultaneously provided toxicity profiles for nine compounds and their metabolites from CYP1A2, CYP2D6, and CYP3A4 and a mixture of the three P450s designed to emulate the human liver. The DataChip-MetaChip system has opened up opportunities for rapid, inexpensive and convenient evaluation of in vitro toxicity. This system will also enable chemical companies to comply with new legislation stipulating that chemicals undergo toxicity analysis, especially European cosmetics companies will be restricted from animal testing starting in 2009, making the need for new in vitro methods even more important. Our discovery has created considerable excitement in both the popular media (1,2) and in the scientific community (3,4). We are now adapting our high-throughput platform for in situ enzymatic modification of drug candidates, and also understanding the mechanisms of drug action.
